## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM-Based Classification (Groq / llama-3.3-70b-versatile)

**What it measures:** The model reads the submitted text and assesses whether it reads as human- or AI-generated based on semantic coherence, stylistic consistency, and writing patterns. It's prompted to return a score between 0.0 and 1.0, where 1.0 means high confidence the text is AI-generated.

**Why this differs between human and AI writing:** AI-generated text tends toward consistent tone, smooth transitions, and low semantic surprise. Human writing carries idiosyncratic word choices, tonal shifts, and structural decisions that don't optimize for coherence. The LLM picks up on these holistic patterns — things that are hard to articulate as rules but recognizable in aggregate.

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0 (e.g., `0.82`), parsed from the model's structured JSON response.

**Blind spots:** The LLM was itself trained on massive text corpora and may have absorbed biases about what "good writing" looks like. It can misread highly polished human prose as AI-generated, and it may underrate AI text that was heavily edited by a human. It also struggles with very short submissions where there's not enough signal.

---

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics (Pure Python)

**What it measures:** Statistical properties of the text's structure — specifically:
- **Sentence length variance**: standard deviation of word counts per sentence. AI text is more uniform; human writing fluctuates more.
- **Type-token ratio (TTR)**: unique words divided by total words. AI text reuses vocabulary more predictably.
- **Punctuation density**: punctuation marks per 100 words. Human writers use dashes, ellipses, semicolons idiosyncratically; AI output is more conservative.
- **Average sentence complexity**: average number of clauses per sentence (approximated by comma and conjunction count).

Each sub-measure is normalized to a 0–1 scale, then combined into a single stylometric score. Low variance, low TTR, and low punctuation density all push the score toward AI.

**Why this differs between human and AI writing:** AI language models are trained to minimize perplexity — they produce statistically "expected" text. Human writing is variable in ways that don't optimize for fluency. These structural signals are independent of meaning and capture regularity at the surface level.

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0 (e.g., `0.61`), where 1.0 means the text's stylometric profile strongly matches AI writing.

**Blind spots:** Poetry, formal academic writing, and deliberately minimalist prose can look AI-generated by these metrics — they share the low-variance, structured quality of AI output. A poet who writes in tight, regular forms will score oddly. Similarly, a verbose AI output with injected variation can evade this signal.

---

### Combining Signals into a Confidence Score

The two signals are weighted and averaged:

```
confidence = (0.6 × llm_score) + (0.4 × stylometric_score)
```

The LLM signal gets more weight because it captures semantic and stylistic coherence holistically. Stylometrics are useful but more gameable and more prone to false positives on legitimate edge cases.

The result is a float between 0.0 and 1.0, where higher values indicate higher confidence the content is AI-generated.

## Uncertainty Representation

### What the score means

| Score range | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| 0.0 – 0.35 | Likely human |
| 0.36 – 0.64 | Uncertain |
| 0.65 – 1.0 | Likely AI-generated |

A score of 0.60 means the two signals produced moderate but not consistent evidence — not enough to call it either way. The system should not pretend otherwise. A score of 0.95 means both signals agreed strongly.

### Asymmetry

False positives (calling a human's work AI-generated) are worse than false negatives on a writing platform. The thresholds reflect this: the "likely AI" band starts at 0.65, not 0.50. A borderline result lands in "uncertain" rather than getting pushed to an AI label.

### Calibration approach

Scores aren't clamped to binary outputs. A 0.52 and a 0.91 produce different labels and different label text. During development, test with known-AI text (GPT/Claude outputs), known-human text (published fiction, personal essays), and mixed content (AI-assisted human writing) to check whether scores cluster where expected.

## Transparency Label Design

Three variants, written out exactly as they appear in API responses and would appear to users:

### High-confidence AI (score ≥ 0.65)

```
⚠️ Likely AI-Generated
Our system found strong signals that this content was produced by an AI writing tool.
Confidence: [score as percentage, e.g. "87%"]
If this is wrong, the creator can submit an appeal below.
```

### Uncertain (score 0.36–0.64)

```
🔍 Origin Unclear
Our system couldn't determine with confidence whether this content was written by a human or generated by AI.
Confidence: [score as percentage, e.g. "54%"]
If you're the creator, you can submit an appeal to provide more context.
```

### High-confidence human (score ≤ 0.35)

```
✅ Likely Human-Written
Our system found no strong signals of AI generation in this content.
Confidence: [score as percentage, e.g. "82% human"]
```

The label always includes the numeric confidence. Non-technical readers need to see that "87% likely AI" is different from "54% unclear" — the number makes the uncertainty concrete.

## Appeals Workflow

**Who can appeal:** Any creator who submitted the content. In this system, the creator is identified by the `creator_id` field they supply at submission. No authentication is implemented beyond that field.

**What they provide:**
- `content_id`: the ID of the submission being contested
- `creator_id`: must match the original submission
- `reason`: plain-text explanation of why the classification is wrong (required, minimum 20 characters)

**What the system does on receipt:**
1. Looks up the original submission in the audit log by `content_id`
2. Verifies `creator_id` matches
3. Updates the submission's status from `classified` to `under_review`
4. Appends a new audit log entry of type `appeal` with: timestamp, `content_id`, `creator_id`, `reason`, original classification, original confidence score
5. Returns a confirmation response with the updated status

**No automated re-classification.** The appeal is flagged for human review. The system doesn't re-run detection on appeal.

**What a human reviewer sees in the appeal queue (GET /appeals):**
- `content_id`
- Original content (truncated to 500 chars)
- Original label and confidence score
- Creator's appeal reason
- Timestamp of appeal
- Current status (`under_review`)

## Anticipated Edge Cases

**1. Polished human prose scored as AI-generated**
A writer who favors clean, spare sentences — think minimalist literary fiction — will produce low sentence-length variance and restrained punctuation. The stylometric signal will push their score toward AI, potentially into the uncertain or even AI-likely band. The LLM signal may also read their consistency as machine-like. This is the most likely false-positive scenario on a writing platform.

**2. AI-generated text that was heavily human-edited**
A creator who generates a draft with an AI tool and then rewrites it substantially — changing vocabulary, restructuring sentences, injecting personal asides — can produce text that neither signal flags reliably. The stylometric score may drop toward human (because of added variance) and the LLM may read the mixed register as human idiosyncrasy. The system will likely score this as uncertain or human. That's arguably correct behavior, but it means the system doesn't catch the "spirit" of the policy.

**3. Very short submissions (under 50 words)**
A haiku or a one-paragraph flash fiction piece gives the stylometric signal almost no data. Sentence length variance over three sentences is meaningless. TTR on 30 words is noisy. The LLM signal can still function, but confidence should be treated as inherently lower for short submissions. Edge case to handle explicitly: if word count < 50, cap maximum confidence at 0.70 regardless of signal outputs.

### Narrative

A submitted piece of text passes through two independent detection signals in parallel — an LLM classification call and a stylometric heuristics function — whose outputs are weighted and combined into a single confidence score. That score maps to one of three transparency label variants, which are returned in the API response alongside a structured audit log entry. When a creator appeals, the system looks up the original audit entry, flips the status to `under_review`, logs the appeal reason alongside the original decision, and returns a confirmation — no re-classification happens automatically.

## AI Tool Plan

### M3 — Submission endpoint + Signal 1

**Spec sections to provide:** Detection Signals (Signal 1 only) + Architecture diagram (submission flow)

**What to ask for:** Flask app skeleton with a `POST /submit` endpoint that accepts `{ "text": "...", "creator_id": "..." }`, calls the Groq API with a prompt that returns a JSON score between 0–1, and returns a structured response with `content_id`, `llm_score`, and a placeholder `confidence` field. Also ask for the SQLite audit log setup (schema: `content_id`, `text`, `creator_id`, `llm_score`, `stylometric_score`, `confidence`, `label_variant`, `label_text`, `status`, `timestamp`).

**How to verify:** Submit 3 test inputs manually — a known AI-generated paragraph, a known human-written paragraph, a very short text — and check that (a) the endpoint returns without error, (b) `llm_score` is a float between 0 and 1, (c) scores differ meaningfully between the AI and human samples, (d) audit log contains the entry.

### M4 — Signal 2 + confidence scoring

**Spec sections to provide:** Detection Signals (Signal 2 + combining section) + Uncertainty Representation + Architecture diagram (submission flow, confidence scoring box)

**What to ask for:** A `stylometric_score(text)` function implementing the four sub-measures (sentence length variance, TTR, punctuation density, sentence complexity), normalized to 0–1. Then the `combine_scores(llm_score, stylometric_score)` function using the 0.6/0.4 weighting. Wire both into the `/submit` endpoint and update the audit log to record both signal scores.

**How to verify:** Run the same 3 test inputs from M3. Check that (a) `stylometric_score` values are plausible (uniform AI text should score higher than varied human text), (b) combined `confidence` scores differ between the AI and human samples, (c) a borderline input lands in the 0.36–0.64 range, not forced to one extreme. Also verify the short-text cap: a 30-word input should not return confidence above 0.70.

### M5 — Production layer (labels, appeals, rate limiting, audit endpoint)

**Spec sections to provide:** Transparency Label Design (all three variants, exact text) + Appeals Workflow + Architecture diagram (both flows)

**What to ask for:**
1. A `generate_label(confidence)` function that returns `{ label_variant, label_text }` for all three bands, using the exact label text from the spec.
2. A `POST /appeal` endpoint matching the appeals workflow spec — lookup, creator_id verification, status update, audit log append.
3. A `GET /log` endpoint returning the last N audit entries (default 20), including both classification and appeal entries.
4. Flask-Limiter configuration on `POST /submit`: 10 requests per minute per IP, 100 per hour per IP.

**How to verify:**
- Hit `/submit` with AI, human, and borderline text — confirm all three label variants are reachable and label text matches the spec exactly.
- Submit an appeal for a classified content_id — confirm status changes to `under_review` in the log.
- Try submitting an appeal with a mismatched `creator_id` — confirm it's rejected.
- Hit `/submit` 11 times in a minute — confirm the 11th returns a 429.
- Call `GET /log` — confirm at least 3 entries are visible with the correct schema.
