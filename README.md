# Provenance Guard

A backend system for classifying whether submitted text content was written by a human or generated by an AI tool. Built for creative writing platforms that want to surface attribution context to readers without policing creativity.

---

## Architecture

A submitted piece of text passes through two independent detection signals in parallel — an LLM classification call to Groq and a stylometric heuristics function — whose outputs are weighted and combined into a single confidence score. That score maps to one of three transparency label variants, which are returned in the API response alongside a structured audit log entry written to SQLite.

When a creator appeals, the system looks up the original audit entry, verifies the `creator_id` matches, flips the status to `under_review`, logs the appeal reason alongside the original decision, and returns a confirmation. No re-classification happens automatically.

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM Classification (Groq / llama-3.3-70b-versatile)

The model reads the submitted text and returns a score between 0.0 and 1.0 representing AI-likelihood. It's prompted at `temperature=0.1` to return only `{"ai_score": float}` — low temperature reduces the chance of the model returning prose instead of JSON.

This signal captures semantic and stylistic coherence holistically. AI-generated text tends toward consistent tone, smooth transitions, and low semantic surprise. Human writing carries idiosyncratic word choices, tonal shifts, and structural decisions that don't optimize for fluency. The LLM picks up on these patterns in aggregate.

**Blind spots:** The LLM was trained on massive text corpora and may read highly polished human prose as AI-generated. It also struggles with very short submissions where there isn't enough signal to assess.

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics (pure Python)

Three structural sub-measures, each normalized to 0–1 and combined into a single score:

- **Sentence length variance** (weight 0.5): standard deviation of word counts per sentence. AI text is more uniform; human writing fluctuates. Low variance → high AI score.
- **Punctuation density** (weight 0.3): punctuation marks per 100 words. AI output is conservative with punctuation; human writers use dashes, ellipses, and question marks more freely. Low density → high AI score.
- **Average sentence length** (weight 0.2): AI text tends toward longer, grammatically complete sentences. Human casual writing runs shorter and choppier.

**Blind spots:** Minimalist literary fiction and formal academic writing share structural properties with AI output — low variance, long sentences, restrained punctuation. These will score higher on stylometrics than their actual origin warrants. The LLM signal typically corrects for this, but it's the most likely source of false positives.

---

## Confidence Scoring

The two signals are combined with a weighted average:

```
confidence = (0.7 × llm_score) + (0.3 × stylometric_score)
```

The LLM gets 70% weight because it captures meaning and style holistically. Stylometrics gets 30% — it's useful but noisier, especially on short text. For submissions under 50 words, the combined score is capped at 0.70 regardless of signal outputs, because sentence-level variance is essentially meaningless over 2–3 sentences.

### Thresholds

| Score range | Attribution | Label variant |
|---|---|---|
| 0.65 – 1.0 | likely_ai | ⚠️ Likely AI-Generated |
| 0.36 – 0.64 | uncertain | 🔍 Origin Unclear |
| 0.0 – 0.35 | likely_human | ✅ Likely Human-Written |

The "likely AI" band starts at 0.65, not 0.50. This reflects the asymmetry in the cost of errors: falsely labeling a human writer's work as AI-generated is worse than missing an AI-generated piece. A borderline result lands in "uncertain" rather than getting pushed to an AI label.

### Example submissions with different confidence scores

**High confidence AI (confidence: 0.67):**
```

society. It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is 
equally essential to consider the ethical implications..."
llm_score: 0.80  |  stylometric_score: 0.37  |  confidence: 0.67  |  attribution: likely_ai
```

**Low confidence / human (confidence: 0.22):**

## Rate Limiting

Applied to `POST /submit`: **10 requests per minute, 100 per day per IP.**

**Reasoning:** A real writer submitting their own work might post a few pieces in a session — 10 per minute is generous for legitimate use and tight enough to stop a script flooding the system. 100 per day covers heavy legitimate use (a platform running batch moderation jobs) while making large-scale automated abuse expensive. The `/appeal` endpoint is not rate-limited separately — appeals are low-volume by nature and require a valid `content_id` that costs a submission to generate.

## Appeals Workflow

Any creator who originally submitted a piece can appeal its classification. The appeal endpoint requires:

- `content_id`: the ID from the original `/submit` response
- `creator_id`: must match the value used at submission
- `creator_reasoning`: plain-text explanation, minimum 20 characters

On receipt, the system looks up the original entry, verifies the `creator_id`, updates the submission status to `under_review`, and appends a new audit log entry of `entry_type: "appeal"` with the reasoning attached. No automated re-classification occurs — the appeal is flagged for human review.

Duplicate appeals on the same content return a 409. Mismatched `creator_id` returns a 403.

A human reviewer can inspect the queue via `GET /appeals`, which returns all appeal entries with the original attribution, confidence score, and the creator's reasoning.

## Known Limitations

**Minimalist and formal human writing produces false positives.** A writer who favors clean, spare sentences — short uniform paragraphs, restrained punctuation, complete grammatical structure — will score high on stylometrics and may score high on the LLM signal too if their prose reads as polished and consistent. This is a property of the signals themselves, not a data problem: both signals are designed to flag regularity as AI-like, and some human writing is genuinely regular. The confidence thresholds and uncertain band exist partly to catch these cases before they become outright misclassifications, but a minimalist writer submitting a tight 100-word piece could plausibly land in the uncertain band.

**Very short submissions are unreliable.** Stylometric variance over 2–3 sentences is statistically meaningless. The 50-word cap on confidence (maximum 0.70) partially addresses this, but a 30-word submission is still going to produce a score that reflects almost entirely the LLM signal with very little structural corroboration. Short-form content — flash fiction, epigrams, social posts — should probably be flagged as low-confidence by default rather than run through the full pipeline.

## Spec Reflection

**Where the spec helped:** Writing out the three label variants in `planning.md` before touching any code meant the M5 implementation was a direct transcription rather than a design decision made under time pressure. The exact wording was already settled. This also made it easy to verify the labels were correct — I could compare the running output against the spec text character by character.

**Where implementation diverged:** The spec called for a 0.6/0.4 LLM-to-stylometric weighting. During M4 testing, the stylometric signal was too noisy on short texts to justify 40% influence — the clearly-AI paragraph scored only 0.37 on stylometrics, which dragged the combined score to 0.627 and landed it in "uncertain" rather than "likely_ai." The weighting was adjusted to 0.7/0.3. The spec's weighting was a reasonable prior; the actual signal behavior on real inputs was the right reason to change it.

## AI Usage

**Instance 1 — Flask skeleton and Signal 1 (M3):** I provided the detection signals section and architecture diagram from `planning.md` and asked for a Flask app skeleton with `POST /submit` and a `llm_classify` function returning a float. The generated code used `request.json` instead of `request.get_json(force=True, silent=True)` — the latter handles malformed input more gracefully and doesn't raise an exception on an empty body. I also added `temperature=0.1` to the Groq call, which wasn't in the generated output, because without it the model occasionally returns prose instead of JSON and breaks the parser.

**Instance 2 — Stylometric signal normalization (M4):** The generated `stylometric_score` function used a std_dev normalization cap of 15, which meant almost all real texts clustered in the middle of the scale and clearly-AI text scored nearly identically to clearly-human text. I overrode the cap to 6, then dropped TTR entirely after it consistently penalized casual human writing (high word repetition) as AI-like. The final signal uses sentence length variance, punctuation density, and average sentence length — a different set of sub-measures than what the AI tool proposed.
